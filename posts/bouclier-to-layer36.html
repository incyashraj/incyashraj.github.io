from bouclier to layer36 — what permissions taught me.

Building a permission layer for AI agents on-chain before building a runtime wasn't accidental. Here's the thread.

Bouclier was not supposed to lead here. I built it because I was genuinely worried about AI agents executing transactions on EVM chains with no meaningful constraint on what they could do. The problem felt urgent and concrete: agents that could drain wallets, that could sign anything their context window told them to sign, that had no principled permission boundary between "things I'm authorised to do" and "things I could technically do."

So I built a permission layer. ERC-7579 as the module standard, five deployed contracts, a full invariant fuzzing suite at 128,000 calls, SDKs in TypeScript, Python, and LangChain. Live on Base Sepolia. The thing worked.

But building it taught me something I didn't expect.

the lesson

On-chain permission discipline is easy to reason about because the environment enforces it. Every state transition is a signed transaction. Every capability grant is recorded on a ledger that doesn't lie. You can't have ambient authority because there is no ambient environment — there is only the state you've been explicitly handed.

Off-chain, all of this collapses. The same AI agent, running as a subprocess on your machine, has access to your filesystem, your network, your environment variables, everything in your process's inherited authority. The careful capability discipline you built on-chain evaporates the moment the agent touches anything outside the chain.

The permission problem isn't on-chain or off-chain. It's everywhere software runs with more authority than it declared.

I kept asking: what would it look like to have the same quality of permission discipline for software in general? Not just agents, not just on EVM chains — all software, on any host.

the connection to karana

Before Bouclier I built Karana OS — an intent-driven OS for IoT devices with a custom kernel, scheduler, and hardware abstraction layer. 200,000 lines of Rust, C, and Assembly. The AI task layer chains device capabilities at runtime based on inferred user goals.

That project was a deep education in what happens when you don't think carefully about the boundary between your logic and the platform underneath. IoT devices run on a dozen different chip architectures with different syscall conventions, different peripheral models, different ideas about what a "process" is. Every hardware abstraction I wrote was a small tax on portability — a place where the logic became slightly more married to one specific host.

Two projects. One pointed at permission. The other pointed at portability. Both kept arriving at the same boundary: the line between what a piece of software is and what the host environment is willing to let it do.

what layer36 is trying to do

Layer36 is the attempt to address both problems at once. WebAssembly as the portable execution format — the same .wasm binary runs deterministically on any conforming runtime, no recompilation per host. UAPI as the single call surface — one dialect for files, processes, and network, translated by host adapters rather than reimplemented per platform. UCap as the permission layer — capability-checked by default, deny everything not explicitly granted.

The Bouclier experience shaped UCap directly. The on-chain model worked because permissions were explicit, unforgeable, and tied to specific resources rather than granted in bulk. UCap is the same discipline applied to a software runtime: capabilities name specific resources, grants are auditable, and the default is deny.

Building Bouclier taught me what a good permission model feels like from the inside. Building Karana taught me what a bad portability story costs over time. Layer36 exists because I couldn't find a runtime that took both problems seriously at once.

All of it led here. Every project was a class on a different part of the same problem.
