notes from UZH — distributed systems, slowly.

What I learned cold-emailing my way into one of the best distributed systems labs in the world — from Mumbai, with no CS degree and no European contacts.

I was a production engineering student in Mumbai. No CS degree. No European contacts. No research background to speak of. I had read a lot, built a few things, and developed a particular interest in distributed systems — in how computers coordinate across boundaries they don't control, how you build reliable systems from unreliable parts.

I found a paper from Prof. Burkhard Stiller's lab at the University of Zurich. The Communication Systems Group — one of the best distributed systems research groups in Europe. I read the paper carefully, found the professor's email, and wrote him a cold email.

A few weeks later I had a fully funded research internship at the lab. First person from my university to ever do this.

what the lab actually taught me

The technical content was important — distributed ledger technology, consensus protocols, the careful thinking about failure modes that characterises good systems research. My thesis on distributed ledger-based technology came out of that period and is being refined for submission now.

But the more lasting thing I learned was about how research actually works. Research is slow in a specific way. It's not the slowness of bureaucracy or the slowness of waiting — it's the slowness of not moving until you understand something well enough to say something true about it. Prof. Stiller ran the lab that way. Papers didn't ship until they were right. Claims didn't get made until they could be defended.

The research discipline was: say less, but mean it. The opposite of how most people communicate online.

That discipline shaped how I think about building. Layer36's threat model and architecture decision records exist because of habits I formed at UZH — the practice of writing down why a decision was made, what it's good for, what it's not good for, what you'd need to see to change your mind.

the Inria offer

Towards the end of my time at UZH, Inria — one of Europe's top CS research institutions — offered me a PhD position. It's still open. I haven't taken it because Layer36 feels more urgent right now than a five-year research programme. But I think about it. Research done slowly and honestly is its own kind of building.

what I'd tell someone starting out

The email I sent to Prof. Stiller was specific. I had read the paper. I had a point of view about it. I wasn't asking to "learn about distributed systems" — I was asking about a particular problem the paper raised and whether there was room to work on it.

Specificity is what separates a cold email that gets a reply from one that doesn't. Not credentials, not connections. Having read the thing carefully enough to have a genuine question about it.

Most doors are not as locked as they look from the outside. Some of them open with a single paragraph, written honestly, sent to the right person.
